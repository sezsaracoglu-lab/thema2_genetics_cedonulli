  • Focus on heredity, genes, chromosomes, prenatal development, and risk vs protective factors including teratogens

🔑 Three Prenatal Periods (Visual on slide)

🥚 Germinal (0–2 weeks)

Fertilisation to implantation. Beginning of dependence on maternal environment.

🫀 Embryonic (3–8 weeks)

Rapid organogenesis. Highest vulnerability period. Neural tube and organ formation.

👶 Fetal (9 weeks–birth)

Growth and maturation. Brain/nervous system refinement. Age of viability ~22–26 weeks.

⭐ Big Picture: The epigenetic view = development results from an ongoing, bidirectional interchange between heredity and environment — neither genes nor environment act alone.
SECTION 2

Genes, Chromosomes & Biological Influences

Genotype vs phenotype · Inheritance patterns · Genetic disorders · Prenatal testing

2Genes, Chromosomes and Individual Differences
🔑 Basic Genetics
  • 23 pairs of chromosomes per cell, carrying thousands of genes made of DNA that guide protein production and development
  • Genotype = genetic makeup (what genes you carry) vs Phenotype = observable characteristics. Environment modulates how genotypes become phenotypes.
  • Genetic mechanisms: Mitosis (body cell division), Meiosis (sex cell division), Fertilisation
  • Inheritance patterns: Dominant–recessive, Sex-linked, Polygenic (multiple genes → one trait)
⭐ Key distinction: Genotype ≠ Phenotype. Same genotype can produce different phenotypes depending on environment (gene–environment interaction).
3Internal Biological Influences: Genetics
🔑 Genetic Disorders & Prenatal Testing

🧬 Chromosomal Errors

e.g., Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome) — extra copy of chromosome 21. Detected by karyotyping.

🔬 Gene-Linked Conditions

PKU (recessive), Sickle-cell anaemia (recessive), Cystic fibrosis (recessive), Huntington's (dominant mutation)

🔁 Recessive/Sex-Linked

Many disorders are recessive — parents may be unaffected carriers. Sex-linked conditions (e.g., haemophilia) carried on X chromosome.

🏥 Prenatal Tests

Ultrasound · Chorionic villus sampling (CVS) · Amniocentesis · Maternal blood screening · NIPD (non-invasive)

⭐ Amniocentesis (shown in slide): needle inserted through abdomen to extract amniotic fluid → tests chromosomal and gene-linked anomalies. Typically weeks 15–20.
SECTION 3

Stages of Prenatal Development

Germinal · Embryonic · Fetal · Critical periods · Age of viability

4Stages of Prenatal Development
🔑 Three Periods (~38 weeks total)
GERMINAL — Weeks 0–2
Fertilisation → Zygote → Morula → Blastocyst → Implantation
Beginning of dependence on maternal environment. Relatively less vulnerable to specific teratogens but exposure may be lethal (all-or-nothing).
EMBRYONIC — Weeks 3–8 ⭐ HIGHEST VULNERABILITY
Rapid organogenesis — formation of all major organ systems (neural tube, heart, limbs, eyes, ears)
Formation of support structures: placenta, umbilical cord, amniotic sac
Period of highest vulnerability to major structural malformations
FETAL — Week 9 to Birth
Continued growth, maturation of organs and nervous system
Brain development and myelination continue
Age of viability: ~22–26 weeks (can survive outside womb with medical support)
Still vulnerable to teratogens causing functional defects (less structural)
⭐ MCQ Key (Q2): Embryonic period (weeks 3–8) = period of highest vulnerability to teratogens causing MAJOR STRUCTURAL malformations → Answer B.
SECTION 4

Internal Risk Factors in Pregnancy

Maternal nutrition · Age · Health conditions · Stress · Foetal programming

5Internal Risk Factors in Pregnancy
🔑 Maternal Internal Risk Factors
  • Maternal nutrition, age, health conditions, and stress can act as internal teratogenic influences affecting foetal growth and brain development
  • Severe prenatal malnutrition (often co-occurring with poverty and poor healthcare): linked to prematurity, neural defects, and later social, motor and cognitive difficulties. Key nutrients: Folate (neural tube), Iron, Protein, Calcium
  • Maternal age: Teenage pregnancy: risk of prematurity, LBW, social complications
    Advanced maternal age: ↑ risk of chromosomal abnormalities (e.g., trisomy 21)
    Paternal age: also influences risk of birth defects and developmental problems
⭐ Remember: Internal risk factors work partly via contextual risks (poverty, poor healthcare access, stress). They are often cumulative.
SECTION 5

External Risk Factors: Teratogens

Definition · Principles of teratogenesis · Critical periods · Environmental toxins · Drugs · Diseases

6External Risk Factors: Teratogens
🔑 What Are Teratogens?

Teratogens = environmental agents or conditions that can cause congenital abnormalities or atypical development. Types: drugs, infections, toxins, maternal conditions.

🔑 Principles of Teratogenesis
  • Severity range: subtle functional impairments → major malformations → death
  • Timing matters: critical periods — embryonic weeks 3–8 = highest risk for structural defects
  • Dose-response: severity depends on dose (amount) and duration of exposure
  • Organ-specific sensitivity: different organ systems have different critical windows (CNS most extended; heart weeks 3–6; palate weeks 6–8)
  • Individual variability: maternal/foetal genotype and physiological status modulate effects
Critical chart on slide: Blue bars show critical periods for each organ. Red zone (weeks 1–2) = prenatal death risk. Red/dark zone (3–8) = major morphological abnormalities. Blue zone (9–38) = functional defects/minor abnormalities.
7Teratogens in the Prenatal Environment
🔑 Three Categories of Teratogens

☢️ Environmental Toxins

Radiation, lead, mercury, pesticides, some household chemicals and pollutants
→ Prematurity, low birth weight, brain damage

🦠 Maternal Diseases

Rubella, genital herpes, Zika, hypertension, diabetes, parasitic & bacterial infections
→ Brain, sensory systems, heart damage, ↑ mortality

💊 Medical & Recreational Drugs

Thalidomide (limb deformities), aspirin, alcohol (FAS), tobacco, opioids, cocaine
→ Physical & neural disruption, neonatal abstinence syndrome

🍺 FAS — Facial Features (slide)

Short palpebral fissures, short nose, flat midface, indistinct philtrum, thin upper lip
FAS = most preventable developmental disability

⭐ MCQ Key (Q3): Exposure to high levels of lead during pregnancy = RISK FACTOR → Answer A. Secure attachment, early childhood education, and high-quality prenatal care are all PROTECTIVE factors.
SECTION 6

Stress, Hormones & Foetal Programming

Maternal stress as teratogen · HPA axis · Long-term outcomes · Dutch Famine evidence

8Stress, Hormones and Foetal Programming
🔑 Maternal Stress as Teratogen
  • Mechanism: Maternal psychosocial stress → ↑ cortisol, cytokines, catecholamines → crosses placenta → foetal stress hormone levels mirror mother's → disrupts brain maturation and HPA axis programming
  • Short-term outcomes in infancy: hyperactivity, irritability, sleep and feeding problems
  • Long-term outcomes: attentional difficulties, behavioural problems, anxiety, depression
  • Historical evidence — Dutch Hunger Winter (famine): Prenatal malnutrition and parental loss linked to long-term associations with schizophrenia, mental illness, and conduct problems (via epigenetic programming)
  • Gut–brain axis: Maternal stress also affects foetal microbiota via the gut-brain axis
⭐ Key concept — Foetal Programming: Prenatal environment can permanently alter (programme) the foetus's biological systems (HPA axis, immune, metabolic) in ways that have lifelong health consequences. This is a form of epigenetic programming.
SECTION 7

Risk, Protective & Promotive Factors

Three-factor model · Five levels (Child, Family, Service, Life events, Societal) · G×E interaction

9Risk, Protective and Promotive Factors
🔑 Three-Factor Model

⚠️ Risk Factors

Increase likelihood of negative developmental outcomes. Examples: teratogenic exposure, poverty, chronic maternal stress, genetic vulnerabilities. Risk factors do NOT guarantee negative outcomes.

🛡️ Protective Factors

Buffer against risks — reduce the impact of adverse conditions. Examples: supportive caregiving, good prenatal care, adequate nutrition, certain genotypes, secure attachment.

🚀 Promotive Factors

Directly foster positive development across ALL levels of risk (not just for high-risk children). Examples: sensitive parenting, cognitively stimulating environments, secure attachment, access to education and healthcare.

⭐ MCQ Key (Q5): Protective factors REDUCE IMPACT of risks; promotive factors ENHANCE POSITIVE DEVELOPMENT for ALL children (not just high-risk) → Answer D. Protective factors do NOT eliminate exposure to risk.
5 Levels in diagram: Child · Family · Service · Life Events · Societal — each with specific risk AND protective factors
SECTION 8

How Factors Shape Developmental Domains

Physical · Cognitive · Socioemotional · Biology × Social × Psychological Venn diagram

10How Factors Shape Developmental Domains
🔑 Three Developmental Domains

💪 Physical Domain

Brain development, growth, motor skills directly influenced by genetics, hormones, nutrition, teratogens, perinatal complications

🧠 Cognitive Domain

Intelligence, language, attention reflect gene–environment interplay. Prenatal insults (FAS, lead, malnutrition) and postnatal experiences both contribute.

❤️ Socioemotional Domain

Attachment, behaviour problems, anxiety and mood shaped by prenatal stress, neonatal neuroregulation, parenting quality and broader environmental conditions

Venn diagram (slide): Biology (physical health, genetic vulnerabilities, drug effects) × Social (peers, family circumstances, relationships) × Psychological (coping skills, self-esteem, mental health) — all three overlap in determining development.

1
Which statement best reflects the epigenetic view of development?
A.Genes determine development, while environment has minimal influence.
B.Environment shapes development, while genes provide only a basic template.
C.Development results from an ongoing, bidirectional interchange between heredity and environment.
D.Genes and environment act independently and additively on development.
✅ Explanation: The epigenetic view (correct: C) emphasises a BIDIRECTIONAL interchange — genes influence how we respond to environments, and environments influence how genes are expressed. Option A = genetic determinism; B = environmental determinism; D = additive (independent) model. Epigenetics = 'above genetics' — external/contextual factors regulate gene expression without changing DNA sequence.
2
During which prenatal period is the embryo as a whole most vulnerable to teratogens that cause major structural malformations?
A.Germinal period — first 2 weeks
B.Embryonic period — weeks 3–8
C.Early fetal period — weeks 9–12
D.Late fetal period — after 28 weeks
✅ Explanation: The embryonic period (correct: B), weeks 3–8, is the period of rapid organogenesis — when all major organ systems are forming. This is when teratogen exposure causes the most severe structural malformations. Germinal (0–2 weeks) = usually all-or-nothing (implantation failure or no effect). Fetal period = functional defects rather than major structural malformations.
3
Which of the following best illustrates a risk factor for human development?
A.Exposure to high levels of lead during pregnancy.
B.Secure attachment at 12 months.
C.Participation in early childhood education.
D.High-quality prenatal care.
✅ Explanation: Lead exposure during pregnancy (correct: A) is a well-established teratogen — a classic external environmental risk factor. Lead crosses the placenta, accumulates in the foetal brain, and causes neurological damage, cognitive impairment, and low birth weight. Options B, C, and D are all PROTECTIVE or PROMOTIVE factors, not risk factors.
4
Which case best exemplifies gene–environment interaction (G×E)?
A.A child with Down syndrome receives early intervention services.
B.A child with a short 5-HTTLPR allele develops depression only when exposed to high life stress.
C.A child with PKU avoids cognitive problems when kept on a restricted diet.
D.A child of smoking parents develops asthma.
✅ Explanation: G×E interaction (correct: B) = the effect of an environmental factor DEPENDS on the individual's genotype. The short 5-HTTLPR allele (serotonin transporter gene) only increases depression risk in high-stress environments — this is the classic gene-environment interaction. Option A = gene + environment acting separately (main effects); C = gene-environment correlation (changing environment to match genotype); D = environmental effect without specifying genetic moderation.
5
Which statement about risk, protective and promotive factors is most accurate?
A.Protective factors eliminate exposure to risk.
B.Promotive factors only benefit children at high risk.
C.Risk factors always lead to maladaptive outcomes.
D.Protective factors reduce the impact of risks, while promotive factors enhance positive development for all children.
✅ Explanation: Correct answer: D. Protective factors BUFFER against risks (reduce impact) but do NOT eliminate risk exposure. Promotive factors DIRECTLY FOSTER positive development across ALL risk levels — not just for high-risk children. Risk factors INCREASE LIKELIHOOD of negative outcomes but do NOT guarantee them (resilience is possible). This distinction (protective vs promotive) is a key exam concept.
